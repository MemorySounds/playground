WHO WE ARE

Robert is a dancer coming from a breaking background, who hasn’t wanted to do battles since he settled in Paris. Hugo worked as a dancer and choreographer until he settled in Paris, where he decided to step back from stage for a while.

But we both love dance, so we got along in our desire to find alternative ways of practicing it. Thus we started to organize PLAYGROUND sessions.

Robert is also a music nerd, DJ, camera holder and avant-garde art lover. Hugo has worked on multidisciplinary projects at Beaux Arts de Toulouse and Architecture school of Montpellier, loves writing poetry and is interested in art performance.

We both want to broaden our artistic practice to different media, and we wish to merge them into the PLAYGROUND sessions.

NEED FOR A SPACE

We often dance in the regular practice spots in Paris (Hall of Le 104, Gym at la Couronne, outdoor dance deck at La Villette). Shared spaces dedicated for dance practice. Well, we want to practice in a different way so we need a different place.

We feel the need of intimacy, we desire a space to fully immerse into.

So we now book slots in studios (Le Cinq, Shakirail) and invite a few people. Each and every time, we proceed to an ephemeral transformation of the space. We shape it with sounds. We paint it with movements. We craft it with scenography. We decorate it with props and costumes. We remold it according to the mood of the day.

Before anything else, we aim to create an environment where we feel goooooood! Free from the unspoken rules of the conventional practice spots.

WHAT IS PRACTICE

The word practice usually refers to an activity that is carried out with the aim of achieving a result. To give the example we best know, dancers usually practice to refine moves and techniques. More specifically, most breakers practice to prepare for battles, most house dancers practice to dance at house-dance parties, most contemporary dancers practice to create/rehearse for shows. In all of those examples, the intention of the practice sessions is external to the session itself., which is to say, the practice is seen a means to an end.

But what happens to a practice session whenever it is devoid of intention? This is the question we are trying to raise through the PLAYGROUND sessions. There is no productive goal in this practice, no battle to prepare, no show to rehearse, no move to unlock, no skills to improve. We practice for the practice itself. We practice because it makes us feel alive. We practice because we love it.

Every practice is different. We try to listen to our own internal state. To do according to our moods. To do nothing if that’s how we feel. To be the spectator. To be the performer.

It should be mentioned that, of course, PLAYGROUND sessions also have outcomes: it can happen that one’s would develop new technique, create material or even that the session itself turn into an improvised performance. But those are only outcomes, not goals, since we enter the session without - as much as possible - intentions. This is an important point since our current context where hyper-productivity is seen as the highest virtue in modern society.
